# JCS Results Narrative

## Results Framing

The results should be written as evidence that the preflight protocol makes useful computational recommendations, not as evidence that one prior family is universally best. Each case asks what the workflow would tell a practitioner before committing to a larger training campaign.

## Main Narrative Arc

The completed outputs show three recurring lessons.

First, graph-style priors can be useful low-budget regularizers. In quick runs, graph priors improve H=32 rollout on spring-mass, graph-wave, n-body, and the controlled HO lattice. Earlier rMD17 and HO cycles also show large long-horizon gains from graph-style node-wise smoothing relative to no-prior GNN baselines.

Second, attribution requires controls. Across rMD17 molecules, exact molecular graph specificity is weak: graph-style priors improve rollout, but permuted or random graph controls are often close or better. Because the permuted control is spectrum-matched, these outcomes support a generic smoothing interpretation rather than exact node-label topology semantics.

Third, temporal and budget controls can change the recommendation. Calibrated temporal smoothing slightly beats graph on the spring-mass and graph-wave quick runs, so the low-budget graph gain does not require topology. Standard-budget runs show that no-prior or permuted controls can catch up, making some quick gains low-budget-only.

## Case Narratives

### HO Lattice: Controlled Positive Audit

The HO lattice is the clean positive case for topology-aligned latent smoothing. At H=32, the graph prior improves over no-prior and the spectrum-matched permuted graph in the preflight run. Cycle 8 checkpointed audit strengthens the interpretation: graph-prior models have lower H=32 rollout error than permuted and random controls, lower normalized `D_true(Delta H)`, and larger low-frequency ratios on the true graph. This supports a topology-aligned latent smoothing label under the tested high prior weight.

Use this case to show what a positive audit looks like, not to claim universal topology specificity.

### Spring-Mass Lattice: Temporal Smoothing Is Sufficient

The quick spring-mass run initially looks graph-positive: at 5 epochs, graph H32 is `0.5740`, no-prior is `0.6864`, and permuted graph is `0.5841`. However, after adding calibrated temporal smoothing, temporal_smooth reaches H32 `0.5689`, slightly better than graph. The correct preflight recommendation is that temporal smoothing is sufficient for the low-budget gain; graph topology is not necessary to explain the improvement.

At 20 epochs, no-prior is best at H32 (`0.0809` versus graph `0.1048`), so the graph advantage does not persist under the standard budget.

### Graph-Wave Lattice: Smoothness-Like Low-Budget Gain

The graph-wave quick run mirrors spring-mass. At 5 epochs, graph improves over none, but calibrated temporal_smooth H32 is `0.5663`, slightly better than graph H32 `0.5712`. The permuted graph is also essentially tied in the calibrated run. The protocol should classify this as temporal smoothing sufficient or generic smoothing, not topology-specific.

At 20 epochs, no-prior catches up and beats graph, again supporting a low-budget regularization interpretation.

### N-Body Distance: Candidate Topology-Specific Under Quick Budget

The n-body quick run is the clearest candidate-topology-specific preflight result. At H32, graph is `0.5433`, permuted graph is `0.5838`, and calibrated temporal_smooth is `0.6453`. Graph also improves substantially over no-prior (`0.8216`). Under the tested quick budget and distance-kNN graph, the recommendation is to carry the graph prior forward and run additional audits/seeds before making stronger claims.

At 20 epochs, graph still beats no-prior but loses to the permuted graph, so the topology-specific interpretation does not persist without qualification.

### METR-LA Correlation Graph: Raw Alignment Does Not Ensure Utility

The METR-LA correlation graph is smoother than permuted and random controls in raw temporal changes, but this does not translate into rollout gain. In the main T=2000/train160 run, no-prior H32 is `0.4068`, graph is `0.4461`, and permuted graph is `0.4337`. The preflight recommendation is to skip this graph prior under the tested condition or revise the graph/model choice before larger runs.

This case is useful for explaining why raw graph-dynamics diagnostics are not sufficient decision rules.

### Graph Heat Lattice: True Generator Graph Can Still Fail

The heat lattice case is a cautionary example. Even with a true generator graph, graph-prior rollout is worse than no-prior and permuted control. This supports a key manuscript boundary: the preflight protocol tests whether a prior helps the tested latent model, not whether the data were generated by a graph process.

### Graph Low-Frequency Lattice: Alignment and Training Budget Interact

The low-frequency lattice stress test has strong raw alignment in favor of the true graph, yet the 5-epoch preflight classifies as generic smoothing because graph does not beat the permuted control. Later analysis shows budget-dependent reversals: graph beats permuted at 20 epochs, while permuted catches up at 50 epochs despite graph retaining smoother latent deltas. This case motivates reporting quick/standard/audit modes together.

### rMD17 and Earlier Attribution Cycles

The earlier molecular dynamics cycles support the broader distinction between graph-style smoothing and exact topology attribution. Graph-style node-wise regularization improves long-horizon rollout across several molecules, but exact molecular graph specificity is weak because permuted or random controls often match or beat the true graph. Covariance conditioning does not explain the graph-style gains in those reports, but neither do they justify a universal exact-topology claim.

## Results Takeaway

The preflight protocol is useful because it returns different actions in different regimes:

- use temporal smoothing for spring-mass and graph-wave quick runs.
- run further graph-prior audits for n-body quick mode.
- skip the METR-LA correlation graph prior under the tested condition.
- treat HO lattice as a controlled positive audit case.
- avoid exact topology claims for rMD17 and generic-smoothing cases.

This is the central JCS result: the workflow converts ambiguous prior effects into explicit computational recommendations.
