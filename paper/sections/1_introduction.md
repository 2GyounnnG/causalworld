Latent dynamics world models are widely used in model-based reinforcement learning, planning, and predictive representation learning. Rather than predicting future observations directly, these models learn an encoder into a latent state and a transition model that predicts future latent states. Recent joint-embedding predictive architectures emphasize this latent-prediction view, avoiding pixel-level reconstruction while still learning useful representations \cite{ijepa,lejepa}. A common design choice is to regularize the latent geometry, for example with variance-covariance penalties that prevent collapse and encourage well-conditioned embeddings \cite{vicreg,barlowtwins}. These priors are intentionally structure-agnostic: they treat latent dimensions symmetrically and do not encode which observed variables are physically or relationally connected. This paper asks a narrower empirical question. When the data has explicit relational structure, such as molecular bond graphs, lattice connectivity, or cell adjacency, does using that structure as a latent prior help dynamics learning? When does it fail?

We study this question by comparing three priors in the same latent world-model architecture. The first is no prior, which serves as the baseline. The second is a Euclidean covariance penalty, in the VICReg and Barlow Twins tradition, which encourages the empirical latent covariance to be close to identity. The third is a graph Laplacian spectral prior, which penalizes the quadratic form \(z^\top L z\), where \(L\) is built from the graph observed in the data. The method is deliberately simple: we use a flat MLP encoder and a small latent transition model, rather than a graph neural network, so that changes in performance can be attributed to the prior rather than to encoder architecture. Section 2.3 shows that the Euclidean covariance prior can be interpreted as a special case of a spectral prior on a fully connected uniform graph. The empirical question is therefore whether replacing this implicit uniform graph with the actual graph structure of the data adds value.

We evaluate this question on three domains that differ in graph stability across dynamics. On rMD17 aspirin, the molecular bond graph is essentially invariant across the trajectory. With 10 seeds, the spectral prior reduces \(H=16\) rollout error by 51% relative to no prior and by 56% relative to the Euclidean prior; it also reduces seed-to-seed standard deviation by factors of roughly 3 to 10 across horizons. On ISO17, which contains 113 C7H10O2 isomers, the same effect reproduces in a different molecular corpus: with five seeds per prior, the spectral prior reduces \(H=16\) test_within error by 57% relative to no prior. More importantly, the cross-isomer split shows that the improvement transfers to held-out isomers: the spectral generalization gap is 1.02x, compared with 1.08x to 1.10x for the baselines. Wolfram cellular automata provide a contrasting case in which the relevant graph evolves rapidly across transitions. At the shared weight \(\lambda=0.01\), the spectral prior shows heavy-tail behavior: its median remains near the baseline range, but one of ten seeds diverges at long horizon. The sub-ablation in Section 3.4.3 shows that this is conditional rather than fundamental: with per-step Laplacian recomputation and smaller weight \(\lambda=0.005\), the spectral prior matches the no-prior baseline. Together, these results support the mechanism discussed in Section 4: the spectral prior helps when the Laplacian matches the graph governing the dynamics, and becomes fragile when the prior is too strong or the Laplacian is stale.

The contribution of this paper is an empirical characterization study, not a new regularization technique. First, we provide a three-domain comparison of no prior, a Euclidean covariance prior, and a graph Laplacian prior for latent dynamics learning. Second, we identify a mechanism-level pattern: sensitivity to Laplacian construction scales with how quickly the graph changes, rather than with a coarse continuous-versus-discrete domain distinction. Third, we extract a practical recipe. When the graph is approximately stationary across transitions, as in molecular bond graphs, several reasonable Laplacian constructions work. When the graph evolves with the dynamics, per-step recomputation and smaller prior weights are required. We do not claim novelty for graph Laplacian regularization, which is classical \cite{belkin2003}; we do not claim state-of-the-art performance on molecular or cellular automaton benchmarks; and we do not claim that the prior generalizes beyond the settings studied here.
