Latent dynamics world models often regularize latent geometry with structure-agnostic priors, such as Euclidean variance-covariance constraints. This paper asks whether explicit graph structure in the data can be used as a more appropriate latent prior for dynamics learning, and when such a prior becomes fragile. We compare three priors--none, a Euclidean covariance penalty, and a graph Laplacian quadratic penalty--in a fixed flat-MLP world model, noting that the Euclidean prior is a special case of the Laplacian prior on a uniform fully connected graph. On rMD17 aspirin (\(n=10\)), the spectral Laplacian prior reduces \(H=16\) rollout error by 51% relative to no prior and 56% relative to the Euclidean prior. On ISO17 (\(n=5\)), the effect reproduces on a different molecular corpus, reducing \(H=16\) error by 57% and tightening the cross-isomer generalization gap to 1.02x versus 1.08--1.10x for baselines. On Wolfram cellular automata (\(n=10\)), the same prior shows heavy-tail behavior at the shared weight, with one catastrophic seed, but becomes stable under smaller weight and per-step Laplacian recomputation. Sub-ablations suggest that sensitivity to Laplacian construction tracks graph evolution rate rather than domain type. The contribution is an empirical characterization, not a new regularizer or state-of-the-art benchmark result.
