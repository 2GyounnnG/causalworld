# Draft Introduction

Node-wise physical and scientific dynamics models often combine learned latent state evolution with domain-motivated priors. In molecular dynamics, coupled oscillator systems, particle simulations, traffic networks, and graph-discretized physical fields, the data can frequently be represented as trajectories `X_t` over `N` interacting nodes. A practitioner may therefore have access to a plausible candidate graph, a temporal smoothness assumption, a covariance structure, or another regularizer before training a full model. These priors are attractive because they can stabilize long-horizon rollout prediction, improve sample efficiency, and encode scientific structure that is not easy to learn from limited data alone. However, their practical value depends on the model in which they are used: the encoder, transition model, latent dimension, prior placement, prior strength, training budget, and rollout horizon can all change whether a prior is helpful.

Graph-prior improvements are especially difficult to interpret. A Laplacian penalty on learned node-wise states may improve prediction because the candidate graph reflects meaningful physical or scientific topology. The same improvement may also arise because the penalty supplies generic graph-frequency smoothing, because a spectrum-matched graph with permuted node labels would have worked just as well, because a simpler graph-free temporal smoothness prior would have regularized the latent trajectory, or because the benefit appears only at a short training budget before an unconstrained model catches up. Architecture can further confound attribution: a graph-aware encoder or transition model may already encode part of the relational inductive bias, while the auxiliary prior acts only as an additional regularizer. For these reasons, a rollout gain from a candidate graph prior is not sufficient evidence that the model used the intended scientific topology.

Full-scale sweeps over priors, prior weights, graph constructions, seeds, and training budgets are costly. They can also produce ambiguous conclusions if the comparison set is incomplete. In computational science workflows, the immediate decision is often not whether a prior is universally correct, but whether it is worth spending additional compute on a larger run, whether a cheaper temporal regularizer is enough, or whether more attribution controls and latent audits are needed before making a topology-oriented claim. This motivates a low-cost preflight stage: a short, structured protocol that screens prior utility and attribution before committing to full training.

This paper presents a computational preflight protocol for model-conditioned prior selection and attribution in node-wise scientific latent dynamics. The protocol takes node-wise trajectories, a candidate graph or Laplacian, a fixed latent dynamics model, a prior-strength setting, and a training budget. It then compares no-prior training, a candidate graph Laplacian prior, a spectrum-matched permuted graph control, optional random graph controls, and a calibrated graph-free temporal smoothness baseline. The protocol is organized into quick, standard, and audit modes. Quick mode asks whether a prior helps under a small budget. Standard mode asks whether the effect persists when the base model has more opportunity to fit the dynamics. Audit mode asks whether learned latent temporal deltas become smoother or more low-frequency in the candidate graph basis. The output is an actionable recommendation: skip the prior, use graph-free temporal smoothing, continue with the graph prior and stronger controls, or run latent audits before claiming topology-specific behavior.

The contribution is a computational workflow, not a new graph prior, a universal physics predictor, a causal discovery method, or a state-of-the-art benchmark. The main contributions are:

1. We formulate prior selection as a model-conditioned computational decision problem for node-wise scientific latent dynamics. A recommendation is explicitly tied to the tested encoder, transition model, prior placement, prior strength, training budget, and rollout horizon.
2. We define a control-oriented preflight protocol that separates graph-prior utility from generic graph smoothing and graph-free temporal smoothing.
3. We use spectrum-matched permuted graph controls, with `L_perm = P^T L P`, to preserve Laplacian scale and graph-frequency spectrum while removing node-label topology semantics.
4. We include calibrated temporal prior-strength matching so that graph and temporal smoothness priors are compared at matched initial effective regularization strength rather than at incomparable raw loss scales.
5. We provide quick, standard, and audit modes that translate preflight outcomes into practical computational recommendations before full training.
6. We specify conservative decision labels that prevent over-interpretation of graph-prior gains and make explicit when topology attribution is unsupported.
